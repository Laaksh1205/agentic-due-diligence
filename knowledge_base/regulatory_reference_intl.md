# International Regulatory Reference — UK, EU, India, Global Lists

> Companion to the main regulatory reference (OFAC / GDPR / SEC), added so
> severity grading is not calibrated only against US frameworks. The grading
> logic is jurisdiction-neutral: restriction or revocation of the ability to
> operate > large fine > small fine > procedural notice. Grade a non-US
> enforcement action by the same ladder as its closest US analog.

## United Kingdom

- **FCA (Financial Conduct Authority)** — regulates UK financial services.
  There is no statutory cap on FCA fines, and enforcement outcomes are
  published as Final Notices. Grade like SEC enforcement: a formal FCA
  enforcement action or business restriction is HIGH; withdrawal of
  authorisation (losing permission to operate) is CRITICAL; an information
  request or supervisory review alone is MEDIUM or below.
- **ICO (Information Commissioner's Office)** — enforces UK GDPR / Data
  Protection Act 2018. Maximum fine: £17.5 million or 4% of annual global
  turnover, whichever is higher. Grade like EU GDPR: a top-band ICO penalty
  for systemic failure is HIGH to CRITICAL; a reprimand or small fixed
  penalty is MEDIUM or below.
- **SFO (Serious Fraud Office)** — investigates and prosecutes serious fraud,
  bribery and corruption (Bribery Act 2010 provides unlimited fines for
  companies). An active SFO investigation or charge is graded like an active
  DOJ fraud investigation: HIGH while pending, CRITICAL on conviction or a
  solvency-threatening deferred-prosecution settlement.
- **Companies House compliance** — overdue accounts, strike-off notices, and
  persistent late filings are OPERATIONAL/FINANCIAL hygiene signals: a
  one-off late filing is LOW; a live strike-off (compulsory dissolution)
  notice is HIGH because the legal entity itself is at risk.

## European Union

- **Competition enforcement (European Commission)** — antitrust fines can
  reach 10% of a company's annual worldwide turnover. A formal Statement of
  Objections or an opened investigation is HIGH; a final infringement
  decision with a turnover-scale fine is HIGH to CRITICAL.
- **GDPR (EU-wide)** — covered in the main regulatory reference: up to €20
  million or 4% of global turnover. The same banding applies.
- **DSA / DMA (platform regulation)** — the Digital Services Act allows fines
  up to 6% of global annual turnover; the Digital Markets Act up to 10%
  (20% for repeat infringements). Formal non-compliance proceedings against
  a designated platform are REGULATORY HIGH.

## India

- **RBI (Reserve Bank of India)** — banking and payments regulator. Monetary
  penalties are frequently modest in absolute terms, so grade the *action
  type*, not the amount: a business restriction (e.g., a direction to stop
  onboarding new customers or halt a product line) is HIGH — it caps growth
  and signals supervisory distrust — while a small monetary penalty for a
  compliance lapse is MEDIUM. Licence cancellation is CRITICAL.
- **SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India)** — securities regulator
  (SEBI Act 1992). An order barring access to the capital markets or a
  fraud/insider-trading finding is HIGH to CRITICAL; a routine
  administrative warning or small penalty is MEDIUM or below.
- **MCA / Registrar of Companies** — Companies Act 2013 compliance. Late
  annual filings are LOW; disqualification of directors or striking-off
  action is HIGH.
- **ED (Enforcement Directorate)** — investigates money laundering under the
  PMLA (Prevention of Money Laundering Act). An ED search, attachment of
  assets, or PMLA case is HIGH to CRITICAL, comparable to an OFAC or DOJ
  money-laundering matter.

## Global sanctions and debarment lists

- **Beyond OFAC:** inclusion on the UN Security Council Consolidated List,
  the EU consolidated sanctions list, or the UK OFSI list is graded exactly
  like OFAC SDN inclusion — CRITICAL, a legal prohibition rather than a
  reputational concern.
- **World Bank debarment:** a firm on the World Bank's debarred list is
  ineligible for World Bank–financed contracts, typically following fraud or
  corruption findings — HIGH to CRITICAL for any company that sells into
  development-financed markets.

## Cross-cutting grading notes (international)

- **Scale monetary penalties to the company, not the currency.** Regulator
  fine levels differ enormously across jurisdictions (an RBI penalty of a
  few crore rupees can carry the same supervisory signal as a multi-million
  dollar US fine). When revenue is known or inferable, grade the relative
  impact: a penalty around or above 1% of annual revenue behaves like the
  rubric's "large fine" tier; well under 0.1% is procedural.
- **Operating restrictions outrank fines.** In every jurisdiction, losing or
  having restricted the ability to operate (licence revocation/suspension,
  onboarding bans, market-access bars) is more severe than a monetary
  penalty of the same headline size.
- **Repeat findings escalate.** A second enforcement action by the same
  regulator for the same failure type moves the signal up one level.
